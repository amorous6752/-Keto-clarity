How your body shifts fuel sources

A simplified view of how the body's primary fuel source shifts as carbohydrate intake drops.

The fundamentals

What is keto, really?

The ketogenic diet is a way of eating that significantly lowers carbohydrate intake and replaces it with fat, prompting your body to shift its primary fuel source. Here's the process in four steps.

1

Carbs are reduced

Daily carbohydrate intake typically drops to a small fraction of total calories, limiting the body's usual glucose supply.

2

Glycogen stores deplete

With less incoming glucose, the body draws down its stored carbohydrate reserves in the liver and muscles.

3

The liver produces ketones

To meet ongoing energy needs, the liver converts fat into molecules called ketones, an alternate fuel source for the brain and body.

4

The body enters ketosis

Once ketones become the dominant fuel source, the body is considered to be in a metabolic state called ketosis.

Why people try it

What drew people to the ketogenic approach

Reported experiences vary by individual — these are commonly cited reasons people explore keto, not guarantees.

Steadier energy

Many describe more consistent energy throughout the day once they're fat-adapted, without the swings tied to high-carb meals.

Simplified meal structure

With clear food categories to build meals around, planning can feel more straightforward than counting every calorie.

Reduced snack cravings

Higher fat and protein intake often supports longer-lasting fullness between meals for many people.

Mental clarity

Some report sharper focus once adapted, which researchers associate with ketones as a stable brain fuel source.

A flexible food list

Meats, eggs, fish, vegetables, nuts and healthy oils all fit the framework, leaving room for real variety.

A clear starting structure

Defined macronutrient targets give beginners a concrete framework, rather than vague advice to "eat healthier."

Common questions

Frequently asked questions

Straight answers to the questions most beginners ask before starting.

It varies by person, but many people reach ketosis within roughly two to four days of consistently keeping carbohydrate intake low, though it can take longer depending on activity level and prior diet.

Keto isn't appropriate for everyone, including some people with certain medical conditions. It's best to talk with a doctor or registered dietitian before making major dietary changes, especially if you take medication or manage a health condition.

Maintaining adequate protein intake and resistance training can help preserve muscle mass while on a ketogenic diet, similar to other dietary approaches.

Typical foods include meat, fish, eggs, full-fat dairy, non-starchy vegetables, nuts, seeds and healthy oils, while grains, sugar and most fruit are limited.

Some people experience temporary symptoms like fatigue or headaches during the first week as the body adapts to using fewer carbohydrates; staying hydrated and replenishing electrolytes can help.
